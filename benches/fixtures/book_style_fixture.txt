Chapter I

When the bell rang for the noon meal, the rain had already passed over the town, leaving the stones bright and clean. Mrs. Grant closed her ledger, folded the blotting paper, and stood for a moment by the window where the market road bent toward the bridge. "It will be crowded now," she said, though no one in the room had asked.

At the far end of the lane, Mr. Hale was arguing gently with the baker's apprentice about a sack of flour that had split in transit. "No blame," he kept repeating. "Only account for the loss." The apprentice, red with embarrassment, promised to return before three o'clock with a written note. By custom, the note would be entered, signed, and witnessed.

The post arrived late. Three letters were for the vicarage, one for Dr. Wilton, and two for the station office. The largest envelope bore a foreign stamp and a hand that tilted sharply to the right. Miss Elinor turned it over twice before setting it beside the inkwell. She had learned, over years of unanswered invitations and delayed replies, not to guess at good news too early.

"Read this first," said her aunt, pointing to the thinner letter. It contained only a brief line: the committee would meet on Thursday at 4 p.m., and all papers should be submitted by noon. Beneath the line was a second sentence, added in haste: "Please bring the revised map." That sentence changed the whole afternoon.

By one o'clock the clerk had returned with measurements from the river wall. At one-thirty the schoolmaster arrived carrying two rolled charts and a packet tied in blue ribbon. At two, precisely, the station clock stopped, and for nine minutes every appointment in the square depended on private watches, sun guesswork, or stubborn confidence.

No one mentioned the clock again once it resumed. Instead, the talk shifted to smaller anxieties: whether the ferry would keep schedule through autumn, whether the northern road would be repaired before frost, whether the new mill contract would hold. "If it fails," said the schoolmaster, "we shall all pretend we expected it." Everyone laughed, because everyone understood.

Near dusk, children crossed the bridge in pairs, trading marbles, apples, and rumors. One boy declared that a violinist from the capital would perform in the assembly rooms next month; another insisted that the hall roof leaked too badly for any performance at all. Their debate followed them past the post office and vanished into the side streets.

When evening settled, Miss Elinor finally opened the foreign letter. It was from her brother, written in a port city she had never seen. He described a harbor full of masts, a wind that smelled of tar and oranges, and a boardinghouse where the walls were so thin that every argument sounded like it began in one's own room. "Do not worry," he wrote. "I am well, and I am learning patience."

She read the line twice, then once more aloud to her aunt, who nodded and said only, "That is worth waiting for." In the kitchen, someone dropped a spoon; outside, the first cart of the night rattled over the stones. The house resumed its ordinary rhythm, and with it came the familiar comfort of tasks still unfinished.

Before sleep, Mrs. Grant updated the daybook in careful columns: flour adjustment, river wall note, committee summons, map revision pending, post received at 12:47, station clock delayed nine minutes. She drew a neat horizontal rule under the entries and dated the next page in advance, as if order itself might persuade tomorrow to arrive on time.
